Said plainly

One Mac is the brain. It runs a tiny background program and that's where your Claude coding sessions actually live and keep running — even with every app closed.

The apps are just windows. On your phone, laptop, Mac mini — they don't run anything themselves. They show you the brain and let you type into it. Install the app on five devices and you get five windows into the same room. Nothing to set up, nothing to choose.

Tailscale is the tunnel. Your phone at a café normally can't reach your home Mac — firewalls exist for a reason. Tailscale safely fixes that: a private, encrypted tunnel only your own devices can enter. No ports opened to the internet, nothing exposed. To your gear it feels like everything's on the same home Wi‑Fi, even a thousand miles apart.

And it stays perfectly in sync. Each session is one real terminal on the brain, and every window watches that same live stream — so your phone shows the exact same thing as your laptop, character for character, at the same instant. One source, many mirrors.

The question everyone asks

“If I install the app on my Mac mini, my laptop, and my phone — which one is the master?”

None of the apps. The master is the Mac running the server — the brain. Every app is an equal window pointing at it.

Close every app and the sessions keep running on the brain, untouched. Open any app again and you're right back looking at them. The windows are disposable; the brain is the one that matters — and you pick which Mac that is (usually whichever one stays on).

“So where does my code actually live?”

“What if I make the Mac mini the brain — but my repos are on my laptop?”

The work happens on the brain, so your code lives on the brain. Each session runs right inside one of your project folders, on that machine. Want the Mac mini to be the brain? Your repos go on the Mac mini — then your laptop and phone need no code at all, they're just windows.

You're never running the same project in two places and hoping they match. It runs in one place and you drive it from anywhere. So the rule of thumb: pick the brain to be the Mac that holds your projects and stays on.

The magic part — a session survives anything

Normally, close a terminal window and the program inside it dies. Ground Control has a small engine called EZ that changes that: it holds each session's terminal open in the background, separate from any window looking at it.

So you can close every app — shut your laptop, put your phone away — and the session just keeps running on the brain. Open a window again and you snap right back to the same live one. (It's also why every device stays perfectly in sync — they're all attached to the one real terminal, not to copies of it.)

And here's the part everyone loves: every session writes its whole memory to a file as it goes. So even if the brain Mac fully restarts — a power blip, a software update — nothing important is lost. When it comes back, it reopens each session from that file and Claude wakes up with its full memory and picks up exactly where it left off. The running program is disposable; the saved memory is what matters.

“Is it contained? Can people see each other's stuff?”

“If a few people install this, is everyone's information walled off?”

Completely — it's private, per person. Everyone runs their own server on their own Mac. There's no shared central server pooling data. Your server only ever touches your sessions on your machine; your code and conversations never leave your own devices.

The tunnel is per-person too: Tailscale only connects devices signed into the same account. Your phone reaches your Mac; nobody else's devices are on your network. Two people are two separate private networks that can't see each other — and none of it is exposed to the public internet, so only your own signed-in devices can even reach the brain.

The one shared piece — the thing that buzzes your phone — carries no content: just the session's name and “tap to view.” No messages, nothing stored.
